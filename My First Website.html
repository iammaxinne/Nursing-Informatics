10 Rights of Drug Administration

Numerous medication errors can be avoided by being aware of the 10 Rights of the Drug Administration. This will benefit nurses, who are typically in charge of administering medications, by helping them perform this task effectively.

1. Right Drug.

Always check and confirm that the drugs name and form are correct. Be wary of drug names that sound and look alike as these similar-looking drug names could result in mistakes when verbal prescriptions are given.

2. Right Patient.

Ask for the patient's or client's name to confirm if he or she is the patient to be administered the drug. You can also check his or her ID band before giving the medication to prevent errors.

3. Right Dose.

Be cautious of the different doses given to an adult and a child. Check the medication sheet and the physician's order before administering the drug.

4. Right Route.

Check the doctor’s order if the route is through an oral, intravenous, subcutaneous, and intramuscular administration, etc.

5. Right Time and Frequency.

Look at the medication sheet to see when it would be given and note when it was last given.

6. Right Documentation.

Make sure to correctly record the time of administration as well as any observations on the chart.

7. Right History and Assessment.

Get a copy of the client's medical history, including any allergies and prescription interactions.

8. Drug approach and Right to Refuse.

Give the client the right to decline the drug after thoroughly explaining the effects.

9. Right Drug-Drug Interaction and Evaluation.

Analyze the patient's diet and any medications they've taken in the past that might have an adverse reaction to the medication being administered.

10. Right Education and Information.

Give the patient enough information about the medication they will be taking, including expected therapeutic and adverse effects.
